### AREA A — Training Volume & Hypertrophy

**Search 1:** "training volume hypertrophy meta-analysis 2023 2024 2025 weekly sets muscle group"

Key finding: A December 2025 meta-regression (Sports Medicine, PubMed 41343037, link.springer.com/10.1007/s40279-025-02344-w) refines the Pelland et al. framework by emphasising the distinction between direct and indirect sets in quantifying volume. The existing RESEARCH_PROMPT_PRO GOOD example already cites **Pelland et al. (2024)** which is the direct predecessor to this work; the new study extends rather than replaces it. The 10–20 sets/muscle/week range remains the consensus target.

**Search 2:** "weekly sets per muscle group dose-response systematic review 2024 2025"

Key finding: Confirmed the 2025 meta-regression conclusion. A 2023–2024 study (PubMed 39665246) found that progressively adding 4–6 sets every 2 weeks over 12 weeks improved lower-limb strength, but confidence intervals suggested plateau at higher volumes — consistent with the current "diminishing returns beyond ~20 sets" language.

**Confidence assessment:** HIGH — the existing claim and citation are the most current major reference. No update needed.

### AREA B — RPE/RIR-Based Training

**Search 1:** "RPE autoregulation strength training meta-analysis effectiveness 2023 2024"

Key finding: A 2024 network meta-analysis (PubMed 40791980) found APRE, velocity-based training, and RPE were all significantly more effective than fixed percentage-based loading for maximum strength. A 2024 JSCR study found "Autoregulation Does Not Provide Additional Benefits" in some conditions (link: journals.lww.com autoregulation_does_not_provide_additional.2.aspx). Mixed evidence, but RPE autoregulation remains a valid and widely-used approach.

**Search 2:** "RIR accuracy trained lifters systematic review rating of perceived exertion proximity to failure"

Key finding (PubMed 30747900 + springer 2025): RIR accuracy degrades significantly away from failure — RIRDIFF at RPE 9 ≈ 2.05±1.73 reps; at RPE 7 ≈ 3.65±2.46 reps; at RPE 5 ≈ 5.15±2.92 reps. Training experience does NOT improve accuracy (p>0.05 for training age). This validates maintaining the RPE >9 / <7 thresholds in ADAPT_PROMPT since RPE 7 is at the boundary of practically useful autoregulation.

**Confidence assessment:** HIGH — thresholds validated. No update needed.

### AREA C — Deload & Recovery Protocols

**Search 1:** "deload frequency evidence-based training 2023 2024 planned deload performance recovery"

Key findings:
- Bell et al. (2024) cross-sectional survey (PubMed 38499934, Sports Medicine - Open): athletes deload every **5.6 ± 2.3 weeks**, typical duration **6.4 ± 1.7 days**. Consistent with current 4–8 week block structure.
- International Delphi Consensus (PubMed 37730925, Sports Medicine - Open 2023): Defines deload as "a period of reduced training stress designed to mitigate physiological and psychological fatigue, promote recovery, and enhance preparedness for subsequent training."
- 2024 controlled study (PubMed 38274324): a 1-week deload at the midpoint of a 9-week program showed no difference in hypertrophy/power, but slightly lower strength gains vs continuous training. Note: this was a mid-program deload on untrained subjects, not representative of the trained-athlete recovery context in ADAPT_PROMPT.

**Search 2:** "sleep deprivation resistance training performance recovery systematic review 2023 2024"

Key findings:
- 2024 meta-analysis (PubMed 40236824, 45 studies): sleep deprivation negatively affects sports performance (mean change −7.56%). Effect significant across all exercise categories.
- 2025 systematic review (Springer, Sleep and Breathing): 13 studies on strength; some report negative effects, others no significant difference — mixed but trending negative.

**Confidence assessment:** HIGH — `sleep < 7h → 10 sets/muscle ceiling` and `recovery_score ≤2 AND sleep_avg <6 → -40% volume` rules are consistent with the evidence. No update needed.

### AREA D — Competition Peaking & Tapering

**Search 1:** "taper protocol competition peaking meta-analysis volume reduction percentage 2023 2024"

Key findings:
- 2023 meta-analysis on endurance athletes (PubMed 37163550): **≤21-day taper, 41–60% volume reduction** is optimal.
- Competitive weightlifters survey (PubMed 35976755, 2023): taper 8.0 ± 4.4 days, volume −43.1 ± 14.6%. Consistent with the current `<2 weeks → 50% reduction` rule.

**Search 2:** "pre-competition taper volume reduction evidence strength power sports optimal duration"

Key findings:
- For minimal fatigue: **7–10 days with 50% volume reduction** (Mujika & Padilla 2003 framework, still referenced in current literature as foundational).
- General range: 60–90% volume reduction in some endurance contexts; 40–60% for team/strength sports.
- Strength sports: ~7-day taper, final session 4±2 days prior to competition. Intensity maintained or slightly reduced.

**Analysis:** The current code rules:
- `<4 weeks`: shift toward sport-specific power, reduce gym volume → consistent with pre-peak intensification phase.
- `<2 weeks`: begin taper, reduce volume 50%, maintain intensity → falls within the 41–60% optimal range. Validated.

**Confidence assessment:** HIGH — both the <4 week and <2 week windows, and 50% volume reduction, are consistent with multiple recent reviews. No update needed.

**Analysis:** The current description emphasises "injury-resilient posterior chain" and "plyometric capacity" for performance — not specifically injury prevention programs. The null prevention finding doesn't invalidate the performance-based rationale. **No change.**

### AREA F — Periodization Models

**Search 1:** "block vs undulating periodization meta-analysis 2023 2024 2025"

Key findings:
- 2025 sprint study: block periodization showed better 40-yard dash improvements vs undulating.
- No consensus: results depend on specific outcome (strength, power, sprint, hypertrophy).
- Both block and undulating remain valid; choice depends on athlete needs.

**Search 2:** "concurrent training interference effect strength endurance meta-analysis 2023 2024 2025"

Key findings:
- 2025 umbrella review (Sports Medicine, Springer, 17 meta-analyses, 144 studies): "comparable improvements in aerobic capacity for concurrent training and endurance-only training."
- Interference effect: "small for lower-body strength in males, absent in females."
- Interference is reduced in trained individuals and when using cycling (vs running) as the endurance mode.

**Assessment:** The current periodization structure in PLAN_PROMPT_PRO ("accumulation → intensification → deload → peak") and PLAN_PROMPT_ELITE ("GPP → sport-specific → pre-competition → taper → peak") are well-aligned with current evidence. The concurrent training interference finding (sex-dependent, training-status-dependent) is an interesting nuance for CrossFit and other concurrent-sport contexts but doesn't warrant a change to the periodization language. **No change.**
