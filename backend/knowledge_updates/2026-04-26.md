### Change 2 — Cycling SPORT_DEMANDS mechanism update (MEDIUM confidence)

**File**: `backend/tiers.py`, `SPORT_DEMANDS["cycling"]`  
**What changed**: Clarified the mechanism by which strength training transfers to cycling performance.

**Before**: "...how strength training transfers to cycling power."  
**After**: "...how heavy strength training (≥80% 1RM) transfers to cycling performance primarily through cycling efficiency and anaerobic power gains — not VO2max or metabolic adaptations — making maximal strength emphasis more effective than hypertrophy-focused gym work for endurance cyclists."

**Why**: A 2025 systematic review with meta-analysis (indexed through February 2025) confirmed that heavy strength training improves cycling performance (time to exhaustion, time trial) primarily through cycling efficiency and anaerobic power — not VO2max or maximal metabolic steady state (MMSS), which were unaffected. This means ≥80% 1RM approaches (low-rep, high-load) are more effective than traditional hypertrophy training for cyclists, a practically meaningful distinction for programming. The distinction guides AI personas to prescribe maximal strength work rather than moderate-load hypertrophy training for cyclists, which is a meaningfully different program.

**Citation**: Supplemental systematic review with meta-analysis (European Journal of Applied Physiology, 2025). DOI: 10.1007/s00421-025-05883-2. PMID: 40632222. PMC: 12881108.  
Supporting: Støren Ø et al. (2010), "Maximal strength training improves cycling economy in competitive cyclists." — PubMed 19855311. (Older finding, now confirmed by 2025 meta-analysis.)  
**Confidence**: MEDIUM — supported by one systematic review with meta-analysis indexed through Feb 2025, findings internally consistent with earlier mechanistic research.

## Full Research Log

### AREA A — Training Volume & Hypertrophy

**Searches performed**:
1. "training volume hypertrophy meta-analysis 2023 2024 2025 sets per week"
2. "weekly sets per muscle group dose-response systematic review 2024 2025"
3. "Pelland Remmert Robinson Zourdos 2025 Sports Medicine weekly volume frequency hypertrophy"

**Key findings**:
- Pelland et al. (2025) in *Sports Medicine* is the highest-quality current meta-regression on volume and hypertrophy (67 studies, 2,058 subjects). This is the paper cited in the codebase, but with the wrong journal and year — corrected as Change 1.
- The dose-response for hypertrophy remains a graded curve (more sets → more growth, with diminishing returns above ~20 sets/muscle/week). The "10-20 sets" range in the GOOD example remains accurate.
- A new per-session volume meta-regression (SportRxiv preprint, 2025) explores whether there is a per-session cap, but this has not yet altered the weekly volume consensus.
- Journal of Applied Physiology 2024 study: maintaining volume is as effective as progressive increases in trained males at moderate volumes — suggests the ceiling may be lower for advanced trainees, but the "10-20 sets" range holds.
- **Assessment**: Core claim valid. Citation had two factual errors (journal, year). Corrected.

### AREA B — RPE/RIR-Based Training

**Searches performed**:
1. "RPE autoregulation strength training meta-analysis evidence 2023 2024"
2. "RIR repetitions in reserve accuracy trained lifters systematic review 2023 2024"

**Key findings**:
- Network meta-analysis (2024, ScienceDirect/PubMed 40791980): APRE > RPE > VBRT > percentage-based for maximal strength. All autoregulation methods significantly outperform fixed percentages.
- Scoping review of 31 studies (Journals of SAGE, 2024): RIR scales are feasible and useful for intensity prescription. Accuracy is highest close to failure and in later sets. Trained lifters show acceptable baseline accuracy.
- Springer article (2025): RIR accuracy is already acceptable in trained individuals and improves with practice.
- **Assessment for ADAPT_PROMPT rules**: RPE >9 → reduce load (Rule 1) and RPE <7 → increase load (Rule 2) are well-validated. The specific thresholds are conservative and appropriate. No change warranted.

### AREA C — Deload & Recovery

**Searches performed**:
1. "deload week training frequency evidence-based protocol 2023 2024 2025"
2. "sleep deprivation recovery training performance strength output systematic review 2023 2024"

**Key findings**:
- PeerJ 2024 (PMID 38274324): A 1-week deload at the midpoint of a 9-week program may negatively affect lower-body strength but has no effect on hypertrophy or power. Suggests strength athletes may need to time deloads more carefully.
- Practical review (2025, shura.shu.ac.uk): Optimal deload cuts volume 30-60% while maintaining or slightly reducing intensity. Code uses -40% (within the 30-60% evidence range).
- Sleep deprivation systematic review (PMC 12263768, 2025): Sleep loss negatively impacts strength performance. Mean ~7.56% decrease in exercise performance across studies. Multiple 2023-2025 reviews confirm sleep deprivation impairs strength, power, and perceived exertion.
- **Assessment**: The sleep <7h → 10 set ceiling and sleep ≥8h → 16-20 set ceiling in TRAINING_RULES are directionally correct. The -40% deload volume is at the conservative end but within the evidence range. The dual-condition trigger (recovery_score ≤2 AND sleep <6) is appropriately conservative. No change warranted.

### AREA D — Competition Peaking & Tapering

**Searches performed**:
1. "taper protocol competition peaking volume reduction meta-analysis strength athletes 2023 2024"
2. "pre-competition taper volume reduction percentage evidence powerlifting team sports 2023 2024"

**Key findings**:
- Meta-analysis (2023, PMC 10171681): Optimal taper for endurance athletes: 8-14 days, 41-60% volume reduction, maintaining intensity. This aligns with the <2 week / 50% reduction rule in ADAPT_ELITE_SUFFIX.
- Weightlifters (PMID 35976755, 2022/2023): Typical taper 8.0 ± 4.4 days, volume decrease 43.1 ± 14.6%. Consistent with current rules.
- Powerlifting review (PMC 7552788): Volume reductions 31.6-67% over 7-28 days for powerlifters.
- Team sports: 3-week taper with 20-60% volume reduction improves VO2max and sprint performance.
- **Assessment**: ADAPT_ELITE_SUFFIX rules 8-9 (`<4 weeks → shift to sport-specific power, reduce volume` and `<2 weeks → reduce volume 50%, maintain intensity`) are fully supported. The 50% reduction is within the 40-60% optimal range. No change warranted.

### AREA F — Periodization Models

**Searches performed**:
1. "block periodization versus undulating daily periodization meta-analysis 2023 2024 2025"
2. "concurrent training strength endurance interference effect latest evidence 2023 2024 2025"

**Key findings**:

Block vs undulating:
- May 2025 meta-analysis: Among experienced exercisers, periodized programs (both block and undulating) produce superior strength and hypertrophy vs non-periodized. No significant differences between block and daily undulating for strength. Block may have slight edge for upper-body power in elite athletes.
- The plan prompts use "accumulation → intensification → deload → peak" which is a block periodization model — this is well-supported.

Concurrent training:
- 2023 Sports Medicine meta-analysis (PMID 37847373): Concurrent training causes small interference for lower-body strength in males (not females). Untrained subjects also show some VO2max interference; trained subjects do not.
- 2024-2025 reviews: 6-hour separation between sessions minimizes acute interference. Sequence (strength-first vs endurance-first) has negligible effect on endurance and small effect on strength.
- TRAINING_RULES Rule 15 (other_activities) already accounts for concurrent training by reducing gym volume when external activities are present. Valid and well-evidenced.
