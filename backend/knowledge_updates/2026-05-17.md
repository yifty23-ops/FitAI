### 2. SPORT_DEMANDS["swimming"] — Add ankle plantar flexion as primary kick power driver (MEDIUM confidence)

**File:** `backend/tiers.py`

**What changed:**
Added ankle plantar flexion and ankle flexibility as the primary kick power determinant (>75% of dolphin-kick propulsion), alongside existing hip flexor reference.

**Before:**
```
kick power through hip flexor and quad development
```
**After:**
```
kick power through ankle plantar flexion and hip flexor development
(ankle flexibility drives >75% of dolphin-kick propulsion force —
prioritize ankle mobility and tibialis/calf strength alongside hip flexor work)
```

**Why:**
A 2024 PMC study (Frontiers in Sports and Active Living, "From dry-land to the water: training and testing practices of strength and conditioning coaches in high level French sprint swimmers", PMC10811196) and supporting practitioner literature confirm that ankle plantar flexion range of motion is the primary mechanical driver of dolphin-kick and freestyle-kick speed. The >75% propulsion figure is cited in Binns (2008) and reproduced in applied coaching resources. The current text implicitly captured kick power via hip flexors and quads but omitted the more primary ankle mechanism. For an Olympic-level elite coach persona, this omission could produce dryland programs that over-prioritize hip flexor work relative to ankle flexibility and tibialis/gastrocnemius-soleus development.

**Citations:**
- Arsoniadis G et al. (2024). From dry-land to the water: training and testing practices of strength and conditioning coaches in high level French sprint swimmers. *Frontiers in Sports and Active Living*. PMC10811196.
- Binns RW (2008). Biomechanics of the dolphin kick. [Applied coaching reference — ankle plantar flexion contributes >75% of dolphin kick propulsion.]
- NSCA Coach (accessed 2026): "Beyond the Pool: Improving Swimming Performance with Dryland Training." nsca.com.

**Confidence: MEDIUM** — The >75% propulsion figure is from a 2008 biomechanics study; the 2024 PMC paper corroborates dryland focus on low-volume, high-velocity/force work. Ankle flexibility is consistently cited by elite swimming S&C coaches as a priority.

### 3. SPORT_DEMANDS["mma"] — Add ISSN 2025 quantified weight cut limit (MEDIUM confidence)

**File:** `backend/tiers.py`

**What changed:**
Added the ISSN (2025) position stand's specific quantified limit: acute same-week water cuts must not exceed ~5% of body mass, with documented performance impairment and cardiovascular risk beyond this threshold.

**Before:**
```
evidence-based weight management (prioritizing gradual longitudinal weight descent
over acute dehydration-based cuts to preserve strength and power into competition)
```
**After:**
```
evidence-based weight management (prioritizing gradual longitudinal weight descent
over acute dehydration-based cuts; per the ISSN (2025) position stand, acute
same-week water cuts must not exceed ~5% of body mass — beyond this threshold,
performance impairment and cardiovascular risk are consistently documented in
the literature)
```

**Why:**
The International Society of Sports Nutrition (ISSN) published a formal position stand in March 2025 on nutrition and weight cut strategies for mixed martial arts and other combat sports (PubMed ID: 40059405). Key findings: optimal acute water loss is ~2–4% of body mass within 24 hours of weigh-in; cuts exceeding 5–8% of body mass within 7 days "consistently impairs performance and elevates cardiovascular risk." The current text correctly identified the direction (gradual vs. acute) but the ISSN now provides a specific, evidence-grounded quantitative limit. Adding this makes the elite MMA coach persona more precise and clinically actionable. We use the 5% figure (lower end of the 5–8% range) as a conservative upper boundary.

**Citation:**
Ricci E, Evans M, et al. (2025). International society of sports nutrition position stand: nutrition and weight cut strategies for mixed martial arts and other combat sports. *Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition*. PubMed: 40059405. DOI: 10.1080/15502783.2025.2467909. PMC: 11894756.

**Confidence: MEDIUM** — ISSN position stand is a high-quality institutional document with extensive literature review, representing consensus. The sport-specific quantification is practically meaningful for the elite coach persona.

## All Searches Performed and Key Findings

### AREA A — Training Volume & Hypertrophy (2 searches)

**Search 1:** "training volume hypertrophy meta-analysis 2023 2024 2025 PubMed JSCR"
- Pelland et al. (2025) meta-regression of 67 studies confirms dose-response with diminishing returns. Published in *Sports Medicine*, not JSCR. Fractional set quantification important. → **Changed citation in RESEARCH_PROMPT_PRO.**

**Search 2:** "weekly sets per muscle group dose-response systematic review Schoenfeld"
- Schoenfeld et al. (2017) remains valid foundational work; Pelland (2025) is the most comprehensive current meta-regression. Both show dose-response relationship. Gains continue beyond 20 sets with diminishing returns; current text "diminishing returns beyond ~20 sets" remains accurate. → **No content change needed.**

### AREA B — RPE/RIR-Based Training (2 searches)

**Search 1:** "RPE autoregulation strength training meta-analysis RIR accuracy 2023 2024"
- 2025 network meta-analysis (PMC12336695): APRE ranks highest, RPE second, both significantly better than non-autoregulated training.
- RIR scoping review (PMC11127506, 2024): RIR less accurate farther from failure and at lower intensities — does not affect the >9 / <7 thresholds which operate near maximal effort where accuracy is better.
- **RPE >9 → reduce load 5-10%** and **RPE <7 → increase load 2.5-5%** thresholds remain well-supported.
- → **No change.** Monitoring: APRE as potentially superior autoregulation method for strength-focused users.

**Search 2:** Covered by Search 1 results above.

### AREA C — Deload & Recovery Protocols (2 searches)

**Search 1:** "deload week evidence-based frequency training volume 2023 2024 2025"
- 2024 surveys: athletes deload every 5.6 ± 2.3 weeks. One-week deloads are standard. A 1-week deload during a 9-week program did not harm hypertrophy or power.
- Current deload trigger (recovery_score ≤2 AND sleep_avg <6) is based on compound fatigue signals, which aligns with evidence.
- → **No change.**

**Search 2:** "sleep deprivation training performance strength recovery threshold hours 2023 2024"
- 2025 meta-analysis (PMC11996801): ~0.4% performance decrease per hour awake prior to exercise. Sleep loss defined as ≤6 hours in 24h.
- Current code: sleep < 7h → cap at 10 sets/muscle; sleep < 6h (in adaptation rules) → mini-deload trigger.
- Research defines sleep loss at ≤6h, which aligns with the <6h code threshold. The 10-set ceiling for <7h sleep is conservative but evidence-supported.
- → **No change.**

### AREA D — Competition Peaking & Tapering (2 searches)

**Search 1:** "taper protocol competition peaking volume reduction meta-analysis athletes"
- Bosquet et al. 2023 systematic review (PMC10171681): Optimal taper = 8–14 days; volume reduction 41–60%; maintain intensity and frequency. Exponential taper superior to step taper.
- Current code: <2 weeks → 50% volume reduction, maintain intensity.
- 50% is within the 41–60% evidence range. 2-week window aligns with 8–14 day optimal. → **No change.**

**Search 2:** Covered by Search 1 results.
