- The existing GOOD example in RESEARCH_PROMPT_PRO cites Pelland et al. (2024) in JSCR

- Confidence in no-change: HIGH

**Search 2:** "weekly sets per muscle group dose-response systematic review 2023 2024"
- Confirmed the 2025 meta-regression as the most comprehensive recent work.
  Key quote: "volume and frequency have unique dose-response relationships" and
  "direct vs. indirect set distinction appears essential."
- The 10-20 sets/muscle/week range in the GOOD example is broadly supported.
- Confidence in no-change: HIGH

**Current assertion validated:** `RESEARCH_PROMPT_PRO` GOOD example:

beyond ~20 sets), per Pelland et al. (2024) meta-regression of 67 studies — Journal of

### Area B — RPE/RIR-Based Training (2 searches)

**Search 3:** "RPE autoregulation strength training meta-analysis accuracy trained lifters"
- A 2025/2026 network meta-analysis (PMC 40791980) found APRE and RPE-based training
  were significantly more effective than percentage-based training (PBRT) for maximal
  strength enhancement.
- A 2024 JSCR study found autoregulation produced 7.4% more volume vs. fixed loading
  but no additional adaptations — suggesting RPE's primary value is load calibration
  (avoiding RPE >9 overreaching and RPE <7 undertraining), not volume inflation.
- RPE accuracy: experienced lifters are more accurate, especially closer to failure.
  Accuracy is already acceptable at baseline in trained individuals.
- Confidence in no-change: HIGH

**Search 4:** "RIR repetitions in reserve accuracy trained lifters systematic review 2023 2024"
- A 2025 Journal of Science in Sport and Exercise study (Springer, doi:10.1007/s42978-025-00338-8)
  found RIR accuracy already satisfactory at baseline in resistance-trained individuals.
- Accuracy improves slightly when varying proximity-to-failure, specifically at RIR3
  (not RIR1), supporting the practical utility of 2-3 RIR ranges in programming.
- 2023 study (PMC 37967832): trained males and females showed high absolute RIR accuracy
  for bench press at 1 and 3 RIR; minor tendency for underprediction.

**Current assertions validated:**
- `ADAPT_PROMPT` Rule 1: `RPE consistently >9 → reduce load 5-10% OR reduce 1 set`
- `ADAPT_PROMPT` Rule 2: `RPE consistently <7 → increase load 2.5-5% OR add 1 set`
- `TRAINING_RULES` Rule 10: `stress_level >= 7/10 → prefer autoregulated RPE-based loading`
→ All thresholds remain clinically appropriate. No change.

### Area C — Deload & Recovery Protocols (2 searches)

**Search 5:** "deload training frequency evidence-based recovery 2023 2024 volume reduction"
- A 2024 cross-sectional survey (PMC 38499934, Sports Medicine Open,
  doi:10.1186/s40798-024-00691-y) of strength/physique athletes found:
  deloads average 6.4 ± 1.7 days, integrated every 5.6 ± 2.3 weeks. Training
  frequency stays unchanged; volume reduced via fewer sets and reps.
- A 2024 experimental study (PMC 38274324) found a 1-week deload at midpoint of a
  9-week RT program **negatively influenced lower body muscle strength** but had no
  effect on hypertrophy, power, or local muscular endurance.
  **Important note:** this concerns a pre-scheduled mid-program deload, NOT a reactive
  deload triggered by poor recovery scores (as in the current adaptation rule). The
  finding does not contradict the reactive deload approach.
- Confidence in no-change: HIGH

**Search 6:** "sleep deprivation training performance strength recovery systematic review 2023 2024"
- A 2025 systematic review (PMC 40663194, Sleep and Breathing,
  doi:10.1007/s11325-025-03413-0) confirmed sleep loss negatively impacts muscle strength.
- A 2024 comprehensive meta-analysis (PMC 39006249) of 45 studies found a mean
  ~7.56% decrease in exercise performance across all categories with acute sleep deprivation.
- Effects were significant for all exercise categories with deprivation and late-restriction.
- Confidence in no-change: HIGH

**Current assertions validated:**
- `ADAPT_PROMPT` Rule 4: `recovery_score ≤2 AND sleep_avg <6 → mini-deload (volume -40%)`
- `TRAINING_RULES` Rule 10: `sleep < 7h → ceiling 10 sets/muscle; sleep ≥ 8h → ceiling 16-20 sets`
→ These thresholds are consistent with the evidence base. The sleep-performance relationship
is confirmed. The specific volume reduction magnitudes are conservative clinical decision rules
not directly contradicted by any study reviewed. No change.

### Area D — Competition Peaking & Tapering (2 searches)

**Search 7:** "taper protocol competition peaking volume reduction meta-analysis 2023 2024"
- A 2023 systematic review (PMC 37163550, Sports Medicine) of endurance tapering:
  current evidence supports ≤21-day taper with training volume **progressively reduced
  by 41-60%**, intensity maintained.
- A 2023 Sports Medicine Open study (doi:10.1186/s40798-023-00633-0, Bell et al.) on
  weightlifter tapering: volume decreased 43.1 ± 14.6%, final training session 1.5 ± 0.6
  days before competition; taper duration was approximately 7 days.
- Confidence in no-change: HIGH

**Search 8:** "pre-competition strength taper volume reduction percentage evidence powerlifting"
- NZ elite raw powerlifters (PMC 26670988): volume reduced 58.9 ± 8.4%, final session
  3.7 ± 1.6 days before competition.
- Croatian open-class powerlifting champions: volume reduced 50.5 ± 11.7%.
- Synthesis: real-world volume reductions are 43–59%. The current 50% in the code is
  a point estimate within this range.
- Confidence in no-change: HIGH

**Current assertions validated:**
- `ADAPT_ELITE_SUFFIX` Rule 9: `competition <2 weeks away → reduce volume 50%, maintain intensity`
- `TRAINING_RULES_ELITE_SUFFIX` Rule 18: `competition <2 weeks away → begin taper — reduce volume 50%, maintain intensity`
→ The 50% volume reduction is within the evidence-supported 41-60% range. The 2-week trigger
window is slightly longer than typical strength-sport taper durations (~7 days), but remains
defensible for multi-sport athletes with higher cumulative fatigue. No change.

## Items Monitored (No Change Yet)

These findings did not meet the full change criteria but should be re-evaluated if additional
corroborating evidence emerges:

1. **Direct vs. Indirect Set Distinction (Volume/Hypertrophy):** The 2025 Sports Medicine
   meta-regression (doi:10.1007/s40279-025-02344-w) introduces the concept that direct
   and indirect sets have different dose-response relationships. If this becomes the
   standard framework in 2+ additional meta-analyses, the volume description in
   RESEARCH_PROMPT_PRO GOOD example should be updated to reference both direct and
   indirect set counting.
   — Monitor until Q3 2026.

2. **Cycling — Heavy Strength Training Specificity:** The 2025 meta-analysis
   (doi:10.1007/s00421-025-05883-2) specifically validates heavy/maximal strength
   training over generic "strength-endurance" approaches for improving VO2max, MMSS,
   and cycling efficiency. If a second independent meta-analysis in 2026 reaches the
   same conclusion with sufficient sample size, update cycling SPORT_DEMANDS to explicitly
   say "maximal and heavy strength training" instead of "leg strength-endurance."

3. **Soccer — Sprint Training as Primary Hamstring Prevention:** The 2024 narrative review
   ("If You Want to Prevent Hamstring Injuries in Soccer, Run Fast", PMC 38787003) argues
   sprint capacity is protective against hamstring injury. If this is confirmed in a
   systematic review or RCT with sufficient power, update soccer SPORT_DEMANDS to explicitly
   mention sprint training as a hamstring prevention modality.

4. **Basketball — Microdosing for In-Season S&C:** A 2023 NSCA article describes
   microdosing programming methodology as a time-efficient in-season approach for
   basketball. If 2+ peer-reviewed RCTs or meta-analyses validate this method vs.
   traditional in-season programming, update basketball SPORT_DEMANDS to mention it.

5. **Taper Duration for Strength Sports:** Evidence suggests typical strength-sport tapers
   average ~7 days (Bell et al. 2023; PMC 35976755), which is shorter than the current
   "2-week" trigger window in ADAPT_ELITE_SUFFIX. This is not a contradiction (the 2-week
   window is when the coach *shifts* emphasis, not necessarily a full taper start), but
   the timing semantics could be clarified if elite athlete feedback surfaces confusion.
